Decoding How the Gut Shapes the Brain — From Microbial Metabolites to Neurodegeneration

PhD Researcher  |  Gut–Brain Axis  |  Microbiome Biology  |  Neurodegenerative Disease

The gut and brain are in constant molecular dialogue — a conversation mediated by trillions of microorganisms and the metabolites they produce. My research investigates how disruptions in this axis contribute to the onset and progression of neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's.

Working at the intersection of microbiology, immunology, and neuroscience, I combine mechanistic in vitro models with translational animal studies to identify how specific bacterial metabolites alter neuroinflammation and neuronal integrity. My goal is to translate these mechanistic insights into candidate therapeutic or diagnostic targets — moving from the gut to the clinic.

My scientific journey

My scientific path began with a fascination I couldn't quite name — an instinct that some of the most consequential biology happens not inside the neuron, but in the metabolic and immunological environment surrounding it. That intuition eventually crystallized into a research focus: understanding how the microbial ecosystem of the gut communicates with, and in many cases dysregulates, the central nervous system.

I completed my undergraduate training in [Degree, Institution], where I was first introduced to microbiology and host–pathogen interactions. It was during a summer research experience that I encountered the emerging literature on the gut–brain axis — particularly the striking epidemiological data linking gut dysbiosis to early cognitive decline and neuroinflammatory signatures in Parkinson's patients. That literature posed a question I have been chasing ever since: what are the molecular intermediaries between a dysregulated microbiome and a degenerating brain?

I am currently pursuing my PhD in [Program Name] at [University], working in the laboratory of Dr. John Kirby. My doctoral research focuses on identifying how bacterial metabolites — particularly short-chain fatty acids, secondary bile acids, and other microbially derived small molecules — modulate neuroinflammatory signaling in the central nervous system.

What draws me to this field is not just its scientific novelty — though the gut–brain axis is one of the most rapidly evolving areas in biomedical research — but its clinical urgency. Neurodegenerative diseases currently have no disease-modifying therapies. The gut, an organ we can modulate through diet, probiotics, prebiotics, and targeted interventions, may represent an accessible and underexplored therapeutic lever.

Research focus

Three pillars of inquiry

My research sits at the convergence of microbial ecology, metabolic biochemistry, and neurological disease. Below are the three pillars that structure my scientific inquiry.

🧠

The Gut–Brain Axis

Bidirectional communication via the vagus nerve, enteric nervous system, circulating immune mediators, and microbiome-derived metabolites that enter systemic circulation and cross the blood–brain barrier.

Why it matters: Gut dysbiosis is documented in AD, PD, ALS, and MS — often preceding neurological symptom onset, raising the possibility of a causal gut-originating signal.
⚗️

Bacterial Metabolites & Neuroimmune Signaling

SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate) act as HDAC inhibitors and GPR41/43 ligands. Secondary bile acids activate TGR5 and FXR receptors now known to be expressed in brain-resident cells.

Why it matters: Butyrate suppresses microglial activation; propionate alters dopaminergic tone in rodents. The CNS is actively listening to the gut microbiome.
🔬

Neurodegeneration — AD & PD

Both diseases involve progressive neuroinflammation and protein aggregation (amyloid-β/tau; α-synuclein) increasingly understood to be modulated by peripheral immune inputs and metabolic signals.

Translational angle: Identifying causative microbial metabolites opens the door to microbiome-targeted interventions as adjunctive therapies where none currently exist.

Current work

Dr. John Kirby's Lab — [University]

My doctoral project addresses a central unresolved question in neurodegeneration research: which microbially derived signals are causally linked to neuroinflammatory activation, and through what molecular mechanisms do they operate?

1

Metabolite-driven neuroinflammation mapping

Systematic SCFA and secondary bile acid exposure in primary microglia and astrocytes. Bulk RNA-seq and Luminex multiplex profiling to build a metabolite–neuroinflammation atlas.

2

Gut dysbiosis in neurodegeneration mouse models

Antibiotic-mediated dysbiosis and FMT in 5xFAD and MPTP transgenic mice. Monitoring neuroinflammatory markers, synaptic integrity, and behavioral outcomes.

3

Receptor and pathway identification

Pharmacological antagonism and CRISPR-based receptor knockouts to identify the cognate receptor and downstream signaling cascade responsible for CNS effects of neuroimmune-active metabolites.

Key scientific questions

  • Which bacterial metabolites consistently modulate microglial activation state, and through which receptors?
  • Does experimental gut dysbiosis accelerate protein aggregation or neuronal loss in AD/PD mouse models?
  • Can restoration of specific microbial metabolite profiles attenuate neuroinflammation in vivo?
Research vision

Long-term direction & goals

My long-term scientific vision is to develop a mechanistic framework for microbiome-informed neuroprotection — an evidence-based understanding of how gut microbial communities can be modulated to reduce neuroinflammation and slow neurodegenerative disease progression.

Beyond my PhD, I intend to pursue postdoctoral training with a strong translational and in vivo focus, where I can test microbiome-targeted intervention strategies — including targeted prebiotics, engineered probiotics, and synthetic metabolite analogs — in preclinical models with clear pathways toward clinical investigation.

The gut–brain axis is inherently a multi-system problem. Solving it will require collaboration across microbiology, neuroscience, immunology, pharmacology, and computational biology. I am particularly interested in integrating spatial transcriptomics and single-cell resolution analyses to understand how microbial signals shape the cellular heterogeneity of neuroinflammatory responses.

"Neurodegenerative diseases affect tens of millions of people globally, and current pharmacological approaches address symptoms rather than disease mechanisms. If the gut–brain axis proves to be a tractable therapeutic target — and the emerging evidence strongly suggests it is — then the opportunity to modify the course of these diseases through accessible, microbiome-directed strategies is enormous."
